Embodied Interaction
A Kinesthetic Interface for Real-Time Audio Manipulation via Optical Hand Tracking

Stefanos Biliousis Aalborg University, Copenhagen, Denmark Sound and Music Computing MSc Embodied Interaction Mini Project
Section I

Project Description

This mini project explores an embodied interaction approach for musical control, where the quality of hand movement shapes sound in real time. A webcam-based tracking pipeline captures 3D hand landmarks from one or two hands, and those signals drive a synthesizer whose timbre, pitch, and effects are organized around the categories of Laban Movement Analysis (LMA).

The core design choice is to move beyond a simple “position equals parameter” mapping. Instead, raw joint data is interpreted through Laban-inspired descriptors such as directness, quickness, strength, and continuity, so that the system responds not only to where the hand is, but also to how it moves. The resulting instrument has a pose layer (what the hand looks like), a motion layer (how it is moving), a position layer (where it is in space), and a second-hand interaction layer that modulates effects on top.

Section II

Abstract

This project presents a prototype for real-time audio manipulation based on embodied gesture input. Using MediaPipe hand tracking through a standard webcam, kinematic features are extracted from one or two hands and mapped to a synthesizer. The central contribution is a Laban-informed interaction model, in which the four Effort factors of LMA, namely Space, Time, Weight, and Flow, together with their eight polar combinations (the Basic Effort Actions), are treated as first-class expressive dimensions. Each Effort factor is operationalized as a measurable signal-processing feature and mapped to an audio stage that owns exactly one of them, eliminating parameter contention and preserving a legible cause-and-effect relationship between the mover and the sound. The resulting system demonstrates that Laban categories can function not only as an analytical vocabulary but as a concrete engineering target for designing expressive mappings in kinesthetic instruments.

Section III

Introduction

Gesture-controlled music interfaces have existed since the theremin, but many modern incarnations share a recurring weakness: they bind hand coordinates directly to audio parameters. Raising your hand increases pitch; moving sideways opens a filter. This is functional but brittle, and it treats the body as a spatial joystick rather than an expressive agent. The mover quickly finds that intention does not register in the sound: a slow, deliberate rise and a panicked jerk produce identical sonic results if they cover the same distance.

Embodied interaction design argues that movement is meaningful action shaped by intention, effort, and context (Dourish, 2001; Varela et al., 1991). A performer does not experience their body as a coordinate-emitting object but as a vehicle for qualities such as lightness, force, hesitation, and resolve. If an instrument is to feel alive, it must read those qualities, not only the geometry.

This project asks a practical question: can Laban Movement Analysis, a movement vocabulary developed for choreography and somatic practice, provide a workable framework for designing expressive mappings in a small, technically feasible prototype? The aim is a pipeline in which design language, signal processing, and sound design speak in the same terms, so that the performer experiences continuity between bodily expression and sonic response.

Section IV

Theoretical Foundations: Laban Movement Analysis

Laban Movement Analysis is a descriptive system developed in the first half of the twentieth century by Rudolf Laban and further formalized by Bartenieff, Lamb, and others. LMA is used in dance, theatre, somatic practice, ethnography, and increasingly in human–computer interaction research, because it provides a qualitative vocabulary for movement that is nonetheless precise enough to be annotated, compared, and, crucially here, quantified.

LMA is typically decomposed into four interrelated categories: Body (what parts are moving), Space (where movement goes in the kinesphere), Shape (how the body changes form), and Effort (the dynamic quality of movement). This project is built around the Effort category, which Laban himself also called dynamics, because it speaks most directly to the inner attitude that accompanies an action, namely the very thing a coordinate-based mapping fails to capture.

The Four Effort Factors

The Effort system is organized as four bipolar factors. Each factor has two polar qualities, an indulging pole and a fighting pole, and a neutral mid-range where no commitment is made in that dimension. The four factors describe orthogonal facets of the same movement, so any gesture carries all four qualities simultaneously, each on its own axis.

Space direct · indirect

The mover's attitude toward their path through space. Direct movements are pinpointed, linear, and single-focus, as when reaching precisely for an object. Indirect movements are flexible, multi-focus, and wandering, as when stirring, gathering, or attending to many things at once.

Time sudden · sustained

The mover's attitude toward time. Sudden (quick) movements are urgent, instantaneous, and surprised. Sustained movements linger, drawing out a phrase with a sense of indulgence in duration. Note that Time is not equivalent to velocity in the physical sense; it is the mover's attitude toward time.

Weight strong · light

The mover's attitude toward force. Strong (forceful) movements assert pressure, impact, and gravity. Light (gentle) movements resist gravity, floating and buoyant. Weight is not mass; it is the intentional use of force.

Flow free · bound

The mover's attitude toward control. Free flow is difficult to stop, since momentum carries the body. Bound flow is tightly controlled and interruptible at any instant. Flow governs the continuity of the phrase and often determines its emotional temperature.

The Eight Basic Effort Actions

When three of the four Effort factors (Space, Time, Weight) commit to a pole simultaneously, the resulting combination is what Laban named a Basic Effort Action. Flow is intentionally excluded from the action-naming scheme because Laban treated it as a modifier that colors the continuity of any action rather than its character. The eight resulting actions are gestural archetypes that most people can recognize and perform without training:

Punch direct · sudden · strong
Slash indirect · sudden · strong
Press direct · sustained · strong
Wring indirect · sustained · strong
Dab direct · sudden · light
Flick indirect · sudden · light
Glide direct · sustained · light
Float indirect · sustained · light

In the prototype these eight actions are first-class citizens: whenever a completed gesture commits to a pole on all three factors, the system reports the matching action by name and renders it prominently in the UI. This gives the mover a direct, named handle on their own Effort configuration and turns learning the instrument into something close to learning a small gestural alphabet.

Why Laban for Interaction Design

Three properties of LMA make it unusually well-suited as a bridge between bodily expression and computational audio control:

  1. Orthogonality. The four Effort factors describe mostly independent axes, so each factor can own a separate audio stage in the synth chain without fighting the others. This is the architectural principle the instrument is built on.
  2. Bipolarity. Each factor is a signed axis with a neutral middle, which maps very naturally onto audio parameters that have a clear “off” or baseline value, such as vibrato depth, distortion amount, or reverb wetness.
  3. Qualitative legibility. Because the categories describe subjective movement qualities, the performer can reason about the instrument in terms of how they intend to move rather than which numeric channel is being affected. This preserves the embodied loop: intention, movement, sound, perception, adjusted intention.
Section V

Interaction Architecture

The instrument is organized around four concurrent control layers. Each layer targets a different category of bodily expression and is routed to a dedicated audio stage, so the layers combine additively instead of overwriting one another. The LMA Effort system provides the backbone of this layering: the motion layer is entirely LMA-driven, and the second-hand layer is described in LMA-compatible terms.

Layer 1, Pose (what the hand looks like)

Six discrete hand shapes select the synthesizer voice. A pose is a static configuration of the fingers, such as Open Palm, Fist, Point, Pinch, Peace, or Thumbs Up, and each maps to a distinct oscillator timbre (warm pad, aggressive sawtooth, clean lead, metallic bell, shimmer, retro buzz). In LMA terms this layer sits closer to the Shape category than to Effort: it does not describe movement dynamics but the configuration of the body at a moment. The purpose is to let the mover pick a sonic character before shaping it with motion.

Layer 2, Motion (how the hand is moving)

The four Effort factors are extracted continuously from the hand's centroid trajectory and drive the synthesizer's effects chain. This is where the Laban framework does its primary work: the mover's attitude toward Space, Time, Weight, and Flow is what the instrument responds to. This layer is detailed in the next two sections.

Layer 3, Position (where the hand is)

The hand's absolute position in the camera frame drives continuous pitch and loudness. X maps to pitch across a two-octave range, Y maps jointly to filter cutoff and amplitude. This layer is deliberately not LMA-driven: it provides the direct, theremin-like coupling that gives the instrument its baseline playability. The Laban layer then colors and shapes what this layer produces.

Layer 4, Second Hand (spatial interaction)

A second, optional hand acts as a modulator on top of the playing hand. The distance between the two hands adds reverb; the height of the second hand adds vibrato depth and rate; the pose of the second hand gates a feedback delay. Conceptually this layer extends the Effort model into the relational dimension (the Kinesphere, in LMA terminology) by using the space between the hands as a signed axis for effect intensity.

Section VI

Operationalizing Laban

The central engineering question of this project is how to turn Laban's qualitative Effort vocabulary into signals that can be computed from a 3D landmark stream at 60 Hz. The strategy is to express each Effort factor as a signed scalar in the interval [0, 1], where 0 and 1 represent the two polar qualities and the neutral mid-range sits around 0.5. Each scalar is computed twice: once as a live intensity from a sliding window, so the instrument reacts while the gesture unfolds, and once as a retrospective quality at gesture end, so the Basic Effort Action can be named.

The Motion Signal

Raw tracking from MediaPipe is first reduced to a single focal joint, specifically the middle-finger MCP (landmark 9), which the Laban-for-animation literature identifies as the most stable single proxy for overall hand motion. The focal joint's 3D position is smoothed with a short moving average; from the smoothed position we derive scalar velocity and acceleration time series, which together form the raw material for Effort extraction.

Gesture Segmentation

Laban treats Effort as belonging to phrases of action, not to individual frames. The pipeline therefore segments the stream into discrete gestures: a gesture start is flagged when acceleration crosses an upper threshold, and a gesture end when it settles back below a lower threshold. A minimum duration filter rejects twitches, and a maximum duration cap releases stuck gestures. Each gesture has a start time, an end time, a list of positions, and a list of accelerations. These form the data record from which every retrospective Effort computation is derived.

Space: Direct vs Indirect

Space is operationalized as trajectory straightness. The measure is the ratio between the total path length (sum of inter-frame displacements) and the direct displacement (straight line from start to end of the gesture): 

spaceRatio = totalPathLength / linearDisplacement

A direct gesture has a spaceRatio close to 1.0 because its path is close to a straight line. An indirect gesture wanders and produces a ratio substantially greater than 1. The live version of this metric is computed over a sliding window of recent positions so the instrument can respond to wandering as it happens, not only once the gesture ends.

Time: Sudden vs Sustained

Time is operationalized through a score that combines two cues: gesture duration and the mean absolute acceleration across the phrase. A short duration or a high mean acceleration contributes to a sudden score; a long duration or a low mean acceleration contributes to a sustained score. The winning pole is whichever score is higher and non-zero; ties resolve to neutral.

Using a score instead of a strict dual-gate (which would require both duration and acceleration conditions to agree) was a deliberate design decision after testing revealed that real human gestures rarely hit both conditions cleanly. A performer may execute a short but measured gesture that reads unambiguously as “sudden” despite a moderate acceleration profile. The score-based operationalization honors the intuition that either cue is sufficient evidence of temporal attitude.

For onset shaping, the pipeline additionally uses a predictive Time estimator: at the instant a gesture starts, the acceleration spike alone is used to predict whether the emerging phrase will be sudden. This prediction is fed immediately into the synth envelope, so that sudden gestures actually sound sudden on their first sample, rather than inheriting the previous gesture's envelope and feeling limp on onset.

Weight: Strong vs Light

Weight is operationalized as mean absolute acceleration across the gesture, modulated by a directional bias toward upward motion. In LMA, strong Weight is often associated with commitment against gravity (pressing, pushing, punching), so a gesture that contains upward acceleration is given a threshold bonus when classifying strength. A gesture whose mean acceleration sits below a low threshold is labelled light. Mid-range gestures are neutral.

The live version of Weight is a sliding-window RMS of acceleration, normalized into [0, 1], which is written each frame to the distortion stage of the synth, so that a gesture which feels heavier actually sounds dirtier, mid-gesture.

Flow: Free vs Bound

Flow is operationalized as the root-mean-square jerk, namely the RMS of the derivative of acceleration, computed across the gesture. High jerk indicates many small corrections, which in LMA corresponds to bound flow (controlled, interruptible). Low jerk indicates a smooth, unstoppable arc, which corresponds to free flow.

Jerk is an unusually good proxy for LMA Flow because it directly measures the smoothness of force application, which is exactly the physical property Laban's Free/Bound axis describes. A ballistic throw produces low jerk; a hesitant, micro-corrected gesture produces high jerk. The live version drives reverb wetness inversely: free gestures produce wet, lingering tails, bound gestures produce dry, contained sound.

Basic Effort Action Detection

At gesture end, the three committed qualities (Space, Time, Weight) are concatenated into a triple such as (direct, sudden, strong) and looked up in the table of eight Basic Effort Actions. The triple must contain no neutral values for a named action to be reported; otherwise the gesture is simply annotated with its factor readings and no action name. This rule preserves Laban's original formulation that an action exists only when all three factors have committed.

The currently detected action is surfaced prominently in the UI, and a running tally of actions encountered in the session is displayed alongside the live Effort meters, turning the session into a small kinaesthetic game of discovering and rehearsing the eight archetypes.

Live vs Retrospective Analysis

Earlier iterations of the prototype only computed Effort retrospectively, at gesture end. This produced a disconnect: the mover had to complete a gesture, and then hear the sound update, which broke the expressive loop. The current architecture runs a live analysis pass every frame that computes sliding-window intensities for Weight, Space, and Flow and writes them immediately to the synth. The retrospective pass at gesture end still runs, but its job is now limited to naming the action and populating UI summaries; it no longer reshapes the audio. This was the single most important change in making the instrument feel responsive to Effort.

Section VII

Mapping Laban to Sound

Each Effort factor owns exactly one audio stage. This is the central hygiene principle of the mapping layer: no two factors write to the same parameter, so they never fight each other frame-to-frame, and the mover can learn the instrument by learning four independent associations rather than a tangle of combined effects.

Effort Factor Poles Audio Stage Perceived Quality
Space direct - indirect Vibrato depth & rate Straight paths sound centered; wandering paths acquire shimmer and modulation
Time sudden - sustained Amplitude envelope + percussive onset Quick gestures become transient hits; slow gestures become held, evolving tones
Weight light - strong Distortion drive Gentle motion is clean; forceful motion becomes saturated and harmonically rich
Flow free - bound Reverb wet/dry Smooth arcs linger in space; controlled, interruptible gestures sound dry and contained

Rationale for Each Mapping

Each pairing was chosen because the perceptual character of the audio stage mirrors the qualitative character of the Effort factor:

  • Space - Vibrato. Indirect movement wanders around a spatial target; vibrato wanders around a pitch target. Both describe an unwillingness to commit to a single point. Vibrato depth grows with the wandering ratio, and its rate shifts subtly upward, because an indirect path typically also carries a sense of flutter.
  • Time - Envelope. Sudden Time collapses duration; envelopes collapse attack and release toward zero. Sustained Time indulges in duration; envelopes with long attacks and releases indulge in the sonic equivalent. On a predicted sudden onset, the synth switches to a percussive trigger (brief attack-release) so the gesture actually sounds transient, rather than the held tone that would result from a pure attack-only trigger.
  • Weight - Drive. Strong Weight delivers force; distortion audibly crushes a signal under force. The pairing is about the audible effort of the instrument itself, not merely about loudness, since volume is owned by the position layer. Weight specifically affects timbral density.
  • Flow - Reverb. Free Flow lets sound fall forward; a wet reverb lets sound continue in the room after the gesture is over. Bound Flow interrupts, so a dry signal stops when the hand stops. Jerk-driven reverb closes the loop: the more the hand hesitates and corrects, the more the room appears to close in.

The Eight Actions as Sonic Gestalts

Because each factor owns a distinct audio stage, the eight Basic Effort Actions emerge as distinct sonic gestalts rather than isolated parameter changes. A Punch (direct, sudden, strong) produces a centered, percussive, saturated hit without any user-side configuration; a Float (indirect, sustained, light) produces a wandering, held, clean tone. Rehearsing the eight actions is therefore a natural onboarding curriculum for the instrument: mastering the action vocabulary is equivalent to mastering the eight most legible sonic outcomes.

Second-Hand Modulation Layer

The second-hand layer extends the Effort-driven synth with relational modulators drawn from the space between the two hands. Its design respects the same one-factor-per-stage hygiene: the reverb stage receives an additive boost from inter-hand distance on top of its Flow-driven baseline, and the vibrato stage takes the maximum of the Space-driven depth and a height-driven second-hand depth. The delay stage is owned exclusively by the second-hand pose and is never touched by the LMA pipeline. When the second hand leaves the frame, these modulators decay smoothly to zero, so the instrument returns to pure single-hand Laban behavior without audible discontinuity.

Section IX

Discussion

The prototype demonstrates that Laban Movement Analysis can function as a bridge between design language and signal processing implementation. Rather than being used only as an analytical vocabulary applied post hoc to describe finished works, the four Effort factors and the eight Basic Effort Actions here became explicit engineering targets for feature extraction, mapping, and UI design. The same words, namely direct, sustained, strong, and free, travel from Laban's original texts through the algorithms and arrive at the sound the performer hears.

Two architectural decisions proved especially consequential. The first was the one-factor-per-stage hygiene: by giving each Effort factor exclusive write access to a distinct audio stage, the mapping became predictable and debuggabl. The second was the live/retrospective split: live intensities drive the sound, retrospective qualities name the action. The split respects Laban's original insight that Effort is a quality of the phrase while also respecting the interaction-design constraint that the sound must react within the phrase, not after it.

Limitations remain. The operationalizations are approximations: jerk captures much but not all of Flow, and the score-based Time estimator compromises strict Laban formalism for responsiveness. The focal-joint simplification ignores the body-part category of LMA entirely; a whole-body version would require pose estimation beyond the hand. The current system is also designed for a single performer, so group Effort, which Laban discusses extensively, is out of scope. Finally, the webcam pipeline inherits the usual dependencies on lighting, skin-tone contrast against the background, and occlusion.

Promising extensions include adding Shape descriptors (advancing/retreating, rising/sinking, enclosing/spreading) from torso motion to color the voice layer; using user-adaptive mapping profiles so the four Effort thresholds calibrate to the performer's kinesphere; integrating depth sensing to improve robustness; and exploring a dance-scale version of the system using full-body MoCap where the Body category of LMA can be engaged alongside Effort.
